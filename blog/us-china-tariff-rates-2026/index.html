US-China Tariff Rates in 2026: What Importers Actually Need to Know

The tariff landscape for Chinese imports changed dramatically in early 2026. If you're importing from China, here's exactly what you're paying in duties right now, how to calculate your total cost, and what changes are coming.

Current US Tariff Rates on Chinese Goods (March 2026)

As of March 2026, most goods imported from China to the United States face a combined tariff rate of approximately 17.5% to 35%, depending on the product category. This is the result of three overlapping tariff regimes:

⚠ Important: The widely-reported "82-104% China tariff" from mid-2025 is no longer in effect. The Supreme Court struck down the IEEPA tariffs on February 20, 2026. The rates above are what's currently active.

The Three Tariff Layers Explained

1. Section 122: The 10% Baseline (Temporary)

On February 24, 2026 — just four days after the Supreme Court invalidated the IEEPA tariffs — President Trump invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act to impose a 10% temporary tariff on nearly all US imports. This is a rarely-used emergency trade provision that's limited to 150 days.

Key facts about Section 122:

  • Duration: 150 days, meaning it expires around July 23, 2026
  • Scope: Applies to imports from virtually all countries, not just China
  • Legal basis: Section 122 has a firmer legal foundation than IEEPA for trade measures
  • Rate: Currently 10%, though there's discussion of raising it to 15%

According to the Tax Foundation, the 10% Section 122 tariff raises the weighted-average applied US tariff rate to 10.2% — the highest since the early 1970s.

2. Section 301: China-Specific Tariffs (7.5–25%)

These are the original "trade war" tariffs from Trump's first term, targeting Chinese goods specifically. They were imposed in four rounds between 2018 and 2019 and never expired:

Section 301 ListRateProducts
List 1 (July 2018)25%Industrial machinery, electronics components
List 2 (August 2018)25%Chemicals, plastics, motorcycles
List 3 (various)25%Furniture, auto parts, handbags, textiles
List 4A (various)7.5%Consumer electronics, apparel, footwear

The rate you pay depends on your product's HTS (Harmonized Tariff Schedule) code. Consumer goods like clothing and electronics are typically on List 4A (7.5%), while industrial goods are usually on Lists 1-3 (25%).

3. Section 232: Product-Specific Tariffs (25–50%)

These tariffs apply based on what the product is made of, regardless of country of origin (though they hit China hard since China is the world's largest producer of steel and aluminum):

Section 232 tariffs stack on top of the other tariff layers. If you're importing steel from China, you could face 50% (Section 232) + 10% (Section 122) + 25% (Section 301) = up to 85% in combined tariffs.

How to Calculate Your Actual Duty

Your total duty depends on three things:

  1. What your product is (determines if Section 232 applies and which Section 301 list it's on)
  2. The CIF value (product cost + shipping + insurance — this is what duty is calculated on)
  3. Whether any exemptions apply (some products have exclusions)

Example: You're importing $10,000 worth of consumer electronics (List 4A) from China, with $1,500 in shipping and $200 in insurance.

Line ItemAmount
Product cost$10,000
Shipping$1,500
Insurance$200
CIF Value$11,700
Section 122 (10%)$1,170
Section 301 List 4A (7.5%)$877.50
Total Duty$2,047.50
Total Landed Cost$13,747.50

That's a 20.5% effective tariff rate on your product cost. For every $100 of product, you're paying $20.50 in duties alone.

Product-Specific Rates: The Section 232 Impact

If your product falls under Section 232, the math gets much worse. The Tax Foundation estimates that Section 232 tariffs alone will cost the US economy 154,000 full-time equivalent jobs and reduce GDP by 0.2%.

The categories hit hardest:

  • Steel products (50% tariff): Raw steel, steel pipe, structural steel, steel wire, nails, screws
  • Aluminum products (50% tariff): Raw aluminum, aluminum extrusions, foil, cans, auto parts
  • Automobiles (25% tariff): Complete vehicles, engines, transmissions, body panels
  • Copper (50% tariff): Raw copper, copper wire, plumbing fittings

For these products, the combined tariff (Section 232 + Section 122 + Section 301) can reach 60-85%.

What's Coming Next

The tariff situation remains fluid. Here's what to watch:

  • Section 122 expiry (~July 23, 2026): The 10% baseline tariff is temporary. If it expires without replacement, the average tariff rate drops significantly. However, the administration may seek to extend it or find another legal mechanism.
  • Section 301 investigations: The USTR has ongoing Section 301 investigations into 16+ countries. New tariffs could be imposed on specific products from China and other countries.
  • Bilateral deals: The US has negotiated reduced rates with Japan (15%), Vietnam (20%), Philippines/Indonesia (19%), and the UK (10%). More deals are possible.
  • De minimis changes: The $800 duty-free threshold for small shipments is under pressure. Losing this exemption would dramatically increase costs for dropshippers and small importers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Are the 82-104% China tariffs still in effect?
No. Those were IEEPA tariffs struck down by the Supreme Court on February 20, 2026 (Learning Resources v. Trump). The current effective rate on most Chinese goods is approximately 17.5-35%, depending on the product's Section 301 classification.
Q: What rate should I use for my import cost calculations?
For most consumer goods from China: use 17.5% (10% Section 122 + 7.5% Section 301 List 4A). For industrial goods: use 35% (10% Section 122 + 25% Section 301 List 1-3). For steel/aluminum/autos: add the Section 232 rate on top. Use our free calculator for exact numbers.
Q: Is duty calculated on the product cost or the total cost including shipping?
Duty is typically calculated on the CIF value — Cost, Insurance, and Freight. This means your shipping and insurance costs increase the duty you pay.
Q: Will the 10% baseline tariff expire?
Section 122 tariffs are limited to 150 days, putting the expiry around July 23, 2026. Whether the administration extends it, replaces it, or lets it expire is uncertain as of March 2026.
Q: How does this compare to tariffs on other countries?
China faces the highest combined rates. For comparison: Japan 15%, Vietnam 20%, Canada/Mexico 10% (USMCA goods may be exempt), EU 10%, UK 10%. See our duty calculator for all countries.
