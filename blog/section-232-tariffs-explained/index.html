Section 232 Tariffs Explained: Steel, Aluminum, Autos, and Copper in 2026

If you import steel, aluminum, auto parts, copper, or a growing list of other products, Section 232 tariffs can double or triple your duty costs — and they stack on top of every other tariff layer. Here's exactly what they are, what rates apply in 2026, and how to calculate your real landed cost.

What Are Section 232 Tariffs?

Section 232 tariffs are import duties imposed by the President of the United States under the authority of Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962. The legal trigger is a finding that certain imports threaten national security — specifically, that the US is not producing enough of a strategic material domestically to meet defense and essential industry needs.

Unlike most tariff programs, Section 232 is not country-specific. When a Section 232 tariff applies to steel, it applies to steel imported from Germany, Japan, Canada, Brazil, and China alike. That said, China — as the world's largest producer of steel and aluminum — is hit disproportionately hard, especially when Section 232 tariffs stack on top of China-specific duties.

The Legal Basis (and Why It Survived the Supreme Court)

This matters for importers right now: in February 2026, the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs (the so-called "Liberation Day" tariffs) in Learning Resources v. Trump. That ruling invalidated tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act.

Section 232 tariffs are legally distinct and survived that ruling entirely. They're based on a different statute, with a different procedural process (a required Commerce Department investigation and national security finding). Courts have historically given Section 232 broad deference, and the 2026 Supreme Court ruling did nothing to undermine them.

Bottom line: Section 232 tariffs are here to stay. The Supreme Court ruling that killed IEEPA tariffs does not affect Section 232. These are two separate legal tools.

How a Section 232 Tariff Gets Imposed

The process works like this:

  1. The Secretary of Commerce conducts an investigation into whether a specific import category threatens national security
  2. If a threat is found, the Commerce Secretary submits a report to the President
  3. The President has 90 days to decide whether to act and what action to take (tariff, quota, other measures)
  4. Once imposed, the tariff takes effect — there's no congressional approval required

This makes Section 232 one of the most powerful unilateral trade tools available to a US president. Unlike Section 301 (which requires USTR investigations and formal comment periods), Section 232 can move relatively quickly.

How Section 232 Stacks with Other Tariffs

This is where things get expensive fast. Section 232 tariffs don't replace other tariffs — they add on top of them. The US currently has three major tariff layers running simultaneously:

Tariff Layer Rate Who It Applies To
Section 122 (10% baseline) 10% Nearly all imports, all countries (expires ~July 23, 2026)
Section 301 (China tariffs) 7.5–25% Chinese imports only, varies by HTS code
Section 232 (product-specific) 25–50% Steel, aluminum, autos, copper — all countries

Which Products Are Affected

Section 232 is broader than most importers realize. Here's a breakdown by product category and the key HTS chapters to watch:

Steel and Steel Products (HTS Chapters 72–73)

The 50% tariff covers an enormous range of steel products — not just raw coils and billets, but downstream manufactured goods:

  • Chapter 72: Raw steel — hot-rolled coils, cold-rolled sheet, plate, bar, rod, wire, tubes
  • Chapter 73: Steel articles — pipes, fittings, screws, bolts, nuts, nails, wire products, chain, springs, structural shapes

If your product is made primarily of steel — even if it's a finished consumer good — it may fall under these chapters and face the 50% rate.

Aluminum and Aluminum Products (HTS Chapter 76)

The 50% aluminum tariff covers:

  • Raw aluminum (unwrought)
  • Aluminum bars, rods, profiles, and wire
  • Aluminum plates and sheets
  • Aluminum foil
  • Aluminum tubes, pipes, and fittings
  • Aluminum structures and parts

This particularly affects: packaging manufacturers, construction product importers, automotive parts, and electronics housings. Many products that are "made of aluminum" but classified elsewhere (e.g., a camera body in HTS Chapter 90) may not fall under Chapter 76 — HTS classification matters enormously here.

Automobiles and Auto Parts (HTS Chapter 87)

The 25% Section 232 tariff on autos applies to:

  • Passenger vehicles (HTS 8703)
  • Light trucks (HTS 8704, select)
  • Engines and engine parts (HTS 8407, 8408, 8409)
  • Gearboxes and transmissions (HTS 8708.40)
  • Body parts including bumpers, hoods, doors (HTS 8708)
  • Electrical systems, wiring harnesses

This is a major cost shock for ecommerce sellers importing aftermarket auto parts from Taiwan, South Korea, or China. A 25% Section 232 tariff on top of Section 301 and Section 122 means a 60% combined rate on Chinese-origin auto parts.

Copper (HTS Chapter 74)

The 50% copper tariff applies broadly to:

  • Refined copper (unwrought)
  • Copper wire and cable
  • Copper tubes and pipes
  • Copper fittings (plumbing, HVAC)
  • Copper sheet and strip

This hits electrical contractors, plumbing suppliers, HVAC wholesalers, and anyone importing copper-intensive products. Chile, the world's largest copper producer, is now subject to the same 50% rate as China — this affects sourcing decisions significantly.

Economic Impact

The Tax Foundation's March 2026 analysis provides the most comprehensive accounting of Section 232's economic effects:

Jobs and Employment

According to the Tax Foundation's conventional scoring, Section 232 tariffs cost the US economy 154,000 full-time equivalent jobs in the long run. That breaks down as:

  • 27,000 jobs lost from Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs alone
  • 98,000 jobs lost from Section 232 automotive tariffs
  • Remaining losses from copper, lumber, furniture, and other categories

The job losses aren't in the protected industries — they come from downstream manufacturers who use steel, aluminum, and copper as inputs. Higher input costs make US manufacturers less competitive globally, reducing employment in those sectors.

GDP Impact

Section 232 tariffs reduce long-run US GDP by 0.2% according to Tax Foundation modeling. While that sounds small, on a $28 trillion economy it represents roughly $56 billion in permanently lower economic output annually.

Revenue

On the government revenue side, Section 232 tariffs are projected to raise $635 billion over 2026–2035 on a conventional (non-dynamic) scoring basis. Dynamic scoring (which accounts for economic contraction) would show a lower net revenue figure.

Source: Tax Foundation, "2026 Trump Tariffs & Trade War by the Numbers", March 2026.

The trade-off in plain terms: Section 232 protects domestic steel, aluminum, and auto producers. It raises government revenue. But it costs downstream manufacturers and consumers significantly more than it saves — a classic protection dilemma that economists have studied for decades.

Exemptions and Exclusions Process

Section 232 tariffs are not absolute. There is an official exclusion process, though it's time-consuming and outcomes are uncertain.

Country-Level Exemptions (Quota-Based)

Some countries have negotiated quota-based exemptions where steel or aluminum from that country faces zero Section 232 tariffs — up to a specified volume. Countries with active quota arrangements include:

  • Argentina, Brazil, South Korea — negotiated quotas on steel
  • Argentina, Australia — quota arrangements on aluminum
  • Japan, UK — ongoing negotiations for reduced rates

Once a country's quota is filled for the year, subsequent imports face the full Section 232 rate. Quotas are tracked and posted by the Department of Commerce — check before you ship.

Product-Level Exclusions

Individual importers can file for product-specific exclusions if they can demonstrate:

  1. The specific product is not produced domestically in sufficient quantity, quality, or on a timely basis
  2. The national security rationale for the tariff doesn't apply to this particular product
  3. Domestic industry has no objection (or objections can be rebutted)

The exclusion process is managed by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) within the Department of Commerce. Key facts about the process:

  • Filing: Via the BIS Section 232 Exclusion Portal (online)
  • Timeline: 90–120+ days for a decision (often longer in practice)
  • Retroactive refunds: If an exclusion is granted, importers can receive refunds on duties paid while the request was pending — but you must have filed before the shipment was liquidated
  • No guarantee: Domestic steel producers can object, and BIS can deny without detailed explanation

⚠ Don't delay filing: The retroactive refund provision is valuable, but it only applies if you filed an exclusion request before the entry was liquidated (typically 314 days after entry). File as early as possible — even if you're not sure the exclusion will be granted.

General Authorized Exclusions (GAEs)

The Commerce Department has issued "General Authorized Exclusions" — pre-approved exclusions that apply to specific HTS codes without requiring individual petitions. If your product's HTS code is on the GAE list, you may import tariff-free without filing anything. Check the current GAE list on the BIS website before paying Section 232 duties — you may be overpaying.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Are Section 232 tariffs permanent?
There's no statutory expiration date on Section 232 tariffs. They remain in effect until the President modifies or revokes them, or until Congress passes legislation overriding them. Unlike Section 122 tariffs (which expire after 150 days), Section 232 tariffs don't have a built-in sunset. The steel tariffs, first imposed in 2018, are now in their eighth year with no sign of removal.
Q: Do Section 232 tariffs apply to products made with steel or aluminum — or only raw materials?
Section 232 tariffs apply based on HTS classification, not composition. If your finished product is classified in HTS Chapter 72 or 73 (steel) or Chapter 76 (aluminum), it faces the tariff. If your product is, say, a steel-frame bicycle classified in Chapter 87, it's subject to the auto tariff (if applicable) or no Section 232 tariff at all. This is why HTS classification is critically important — the same physical product can have dramatically different duty outcomes depending on how it's classified.
Q: My supplier is offering to ship through a third country to avoid Section 232. Is that legal?
No, and it's a serious customs violation. Country of origin for tariff purposes is determined by where the product was substantially transformed — not where it was last shipped from. Routing Chinese steel through Vietnam and declaring it Vietnamese-origin constitutes customs fraud and can result in seizure of goods, large penalties, and criminal charges. The CBP actively investigates "transshipment" schemes, especially for steel and aluminum.
Q: I import auto parts from Taiwan, not China. Do I still pay Section 232?
Yes. Section 232 automotive tariffs (25%) apply to all countries — Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, Germany, and China alike. What you avoid with Taiwan sourcing is the Section 301 tariff (which only applies to Chinese-origin goods). So Taiwan auto parts face: 25% (Section 232) + 10% (Section 122) = 35% total. Chinese auto parts face: 25% (Section 232) + 10% (Section 122) + 25% (Section 301) = 60% total. Taiwan sourcing saves you 25 percentage points on auto parts.
Q: The steel tariff went from 25% to 50%. When exactly did that happen?
The original 25% steel tariff and 10% aluminum tariff were imposed in March 2018 under the first Trump administration. In March 2025, the rates were raised to 50% for steel and 50% for aluminum as part of a broader tariff escalation. If your landed cost models were built before March 2025, they're significantly underestimating your duty exposure.
Q: Can the Supreme Court strike down Section 232 tariffs the way it struck down IEEPA tariffs?
This is a real legal question, but most trade law experts consider Section 232 to be on firmer legal footing than IEEPA tariffs. Section 232 has an established procedural framework (mandatory Commerce investigation, written findings, Presidential determination), and courts have historically given it strong deference. The February 2026 IEEPA ruling was specifically about the scope of IEEPA authority — it didn't address Section 232, which is a separate statute with a separate legal history. Legal challenges to Section 232 have been raised in the past and have not succeeded at the Supreme Court level.
Q: As a Shopify store owner importing metal products, what should I do right now?
Three immediate steps: (1) Audit your HTS codes — verify every product's classification to confirm which, if any, Section 232 rates apply. Misclassification is both an overpayment risk and an underpayment liability. (2) Recalculate landed costs using current rates — factor in Section 232 + Section 122 + Section 301 as applicable. Your margins may be materially different from what your spreadsheet says. (3) File exclusion requests if you believe you qualify — the retroactive refund provision makes it worth doing even when the outcome is uncertain.
