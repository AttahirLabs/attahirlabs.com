Customs Broker Due-Diligence Checklist for Shopify Merchants

---

Hiring a customs broker is one of those Shopify growth steps that feels simple until it is not. A supplier is ready to ship. Your freight forwarder says they can "handle customs." A broker sends over a power of attorney. The warehouse wants an ETA. Meanwhile, the duty rate, product classification, country of origin, and importer recordkeeping obligations all sit behind a set of forms most merchants only see after the shipment is already moving.

The mistake is treating the broker as a checkout add-on instead of a compliance partner.

A good customs broker can help you file entries, interpret product data, coordinate release, pay duties, and respond when customs asks questions. But the importer still has responsibilities. In the United States, CBP's reasonable care framework expects importers to provide accurate information and maintain controls over classification, valuation, origin, and documentation. In Canada, CBSA tells importers that even when they authorize a licensed customs broker, they remain ultimately responsible for accounting documentation, duties and taxes, and later corrections.

That does not mean Shopify merchants need to become customs lawyers. It means they need a better onboarding process than "someone recommended this broker in a Slack group."

Use this checklist before you sign a broker power of attorney, before you let a freight forwarder choose the broker without naming them, and before your first high-value import lands at the border. It is grounded in CBP's importer reasonable care guidance[1], the eCFR customs broker rules[2], and CBSA's reminder that Canadian importers remain responsible when they use a licensed customs broker[3].

Quick answer

Before a Shopify merchant hires a customs broker, verify the broker's license, confirm the power of attorney is signed directly with the importer of record, test category-specific classification knowledge, get a written fee schedule, confirm entry-record access, and connect broker data back to SKU-level HS codes, country of origin, supplier invoices, and landed-cost assumptions in Shopify. The broker can file entries and coordinate release, but the importer still needs controls over classification, valuation, origin, and records.

Why broker due diligence matters for Shopify stores

Shopify merchants often start with small parcels, courier brokerage, and supplier-arranged shipping. That can work during product testing. The risk grows when orders move from samples to pallets, ocean containers, DDP claims, multi-country sourcing, or regulated products.

Three things change at that stage.

First, customs data becomes margin data. The HS or HTS classification attached to a product can change the duty rate, special tariff exposure, admissibility review, and landed cost. If the broker uses a generic description or an old code from a supplier invoice, your product margin can be wrong before the goods arrive.

Second, the importer of record is not just a shipping label field. The importer is the party customs can ask for records, corrections, duties, and explanations. A broker can transact customs business on your behalf, but the broker does not magically absorb your importer obligations.

Third, ecommerce teams move fast. Product titles, variants, bundles, origin countries, suppliers, and fulfillment methods change often. Customs compliance breaks when the broker sees only a PDF invoice while the Shopify catalog, purchase order, and actual product composition tell a more complicated story.

Due diligence is how you find out whether a broker can operate in that reality.

The short version: your broker checklist

Before hiring a customs broker, confirm these items in writing:

  • The broker is licensed in the country where the goods will be imported.
  • The broker will execute the power of attorney directly with your importer of record, not through an unnamed third party.
  • The broker has handled your product category, sales channel, and shipment mode before.
  • The broker can explain how they review classification, valuation, country of origin, and admissibility data.
  • The broker gives you an itemized fee schedule before the first shipment.
  • The broker names the actual operating contact and escalation path.
  • The broker can provide copies of entry summaries, release documents, invoices, and correspondence on request.
  • The broker has a record retention process that works with your own recordkeeping obligations.
  • The broker has a written process for customs notices, holds, exams, and post-entry corrections.
  • The broker understands that Shopify product data, supplier invoices, purchase orders, and landed-cost settings need to stay aligned.

If any of those answers are vague, slow, or treated as unusual, keep looking.

Step 1: Confirm the broker is actually licensed

Start with the basic credential check.

In the United States, customs brokers are regulated under 19 CFR Part 111. The rule covers broker licensing, permits, responsibilities, recordkeeping, discipline, penalties, and continuing education. A person generally needs a broker license to transact customs business on behalf of others, while an importer may transact customs business for its own account.

For a Shopify merchant, the practical question is simple: who is filing with CBP on your behalf, and under what license?

Ask for:

  • The broker's legal business name.
  • The individual or corporate broker license details.
  • Confirmation that the broker has authority to operate under the current national permit framework.
  • The name of the broker entity that will appear in customs transactions.

Do not accept "our logistics partner handles that" as the full answer. A freight forwarder may also be a licensed customs broker, may have an affiliated broker, or may subcontract the filing. Those are three different risk profiles. You need the name of the licensed party.

For Canada, use CBSA's licensed customs broker list. CBSA's importing guidance says a business may prepare its own release and accounting documents or authorize a licensed customs broker to act as its agent. It also says brokers are private agents, not government employees. That distinction matters: licensing is official, but service quality, fees, response time, and category expertise are still commercial due-diligence questions.

Step 2: Verify the power of attorney process

A customs power of attorney is normal. It lets the broker transact customs business for you. It is also a control point.

CBP's modernized broker regulations emphasize the broker-client relationship. Under 19 CFR 111.36(c)(3), a broker must execute a customs power of attorney directly with the importer of record or drawback claimant, not through a freight forwarder or other third party. CBP has also published guidance warning that powers of attorney should be examined carefully because they can authorize customs activity involving merchandise entering the United States.

For merchants, the checklist is:

  • Does the legal importer name match your business records?
  • Does the address match the entity that will act as importer of record?
  • Who is signing, and do they have authority to bind the company?
  • Is the POA limited to customs business, or does it include broader language you do not understand?
  • Is the broker asking for identity documents or business verification before accepting you as a client?
  • Will the broker provide a signed copy for your records?

If a forwarder says "just sign our packet and we will route it," pause until the actual broker is named. You want a direct broker relationship, not an invisible chain of handoffs.

Step 3: Test product-category competence

Most broker mistakes are not dramatic. They are quiet: a vague description, an outdated HTS code, a supplier invoice that says "accessory," or a country of origin copied from the ship-from address instead of the place where the product was manufactured.

Ask questions that force category-specific answers:

  • What similar products have you cleared in the last 12 months?
  • What documents do you need to classify this product correctly?
  • What material composition, end use, technical specs, or images would change the classification analysis?
  • Are there partner government agency requirements for this category?
  • Are there antidumping, countervailing duty, Section 301, Section 232, SIMA, or other special-duty issues we should screen for?
  • Do you recommend a binding ruling when classification is uncertain?

The broker does not need to give a final legal opinion on a sales call. They should, however, know what facts matter. If you sell apparel, they should ask about fiber content, gender, construction, and knit versus woven details. If you sell electronics, they should ask about components, radio frequency features, batteries, and safety documentation. If you sell cosmetics, supplements, food-contact products, or children's goods, they should immediately discuss admissibility and agency requirements beyond ordinary duty rates.

For Shopify teams, this is where product operations and customs operations meet. Your product page title is not enough. Build a simple product master record with SKU, supplier, origin, composition, HTS or HS code, product images, purchase price, and any regulatory notes. Give the broker that record before the shipment moves. CBP's reasonable care checklist[1] is useful here because it pushes importers to ask what product facts support classification, value, origin, and admissibility before entry.

Step 4: Review classification, valuation, and origin controls

The USITC Harmonized Tariff Schedule is the official tariff schedule for US imports. It sets out tariff rates and statistical categories for merchandise imported into the United States. Canada uses its own tariff and CBSA import process. The first six digits of an HS code are internationally harmonized, but country-specific tariff schedules and statistical suffixes matter.

Your broker should have a workflow for the three customs fields that most often change landed cost:

Classification. The product code needs to match the actual product, not just a supplier's legacy code. Ask whether the broker uses prior entries, product specs, CBP rulings, tariff notes, and internal review before filing.

Valuation. The WTO customs valuation framework is built around a fair, uniform, and neutral system, with transaction value often central to the analysis. For merchants, valuation questions show up in assists, royalties, tooling, discounts, related-party purchases, freight and insurance treatment, and whether the invoice value reflects the actual sale for export.

Origin. Country of origin is not always the same as the country of shipment. CBSA's importing guidance makes that point explicitly for Canadian imports. Origin can affect duty preference, marking, special tariffs, trade remedies, and customer-facing claims.

Do not ask "Can you classify this?" Ask "What information do you need to classify this defensibly, and how will you document the decision?"

Step 5: Demand a practical communication SLA

Customs issues are time-sensitive. A broker who is fine for a quarterly industrial importer may be painful for a Shopify merchant with seasonal drops, inventory constraints, and customers waiting.

Set expectations before the first shipment:

  • Who is the day-to-day contact?
  • Who is the backup if that person is unavailable?
  • What hours do they monitor urgent holds?
  • How quickly do they acknowledge document requests?
  • How do they notify you of exams, CBP or CBSA questions, rejected entries, or duty changes?
  • Will they communicate with your freight forwarder and 3PL directly?
  • Will they copy you on material customs correspondence?

You are not looking for a fantasy guarantee that customs will never delay a shipment. You are looking for a broker who can tell you what is happening, what they need, and what comes next.

Step 6: Get the fee structure in writing

Brokerage fees are commercial fees. They vary by broker, shipment mode, entry complexity, number of lines, exams, disbursements, courier handoffs, bonds, and post-entry work.

Ask for an itemized schedule covering:

  • Entry preparation and filing.
  • ISF filing for ocean shipments, if applicable.
  • Customs bond setup or annual bond support.
  • Disbursement or advancement fees.
  • PGA or regulated-product handling.
  • Exam, hold, storage coordination, or document correction fees.
  • Post-entry amendments, protests, corrections, or refund work.
  • Record retrieval fees.
  • After-hours or urgent handling fees.

The goal is not to choose the cheapest broker. The goal is to avoid surprise fees and understand when complexity triggers additional charges.

For Shopify merchants, this should feed directly into landed-cost planning. If a shipment has 40 tariff lines, a low headline brokerage fee may not be the actual cost. If duties, brokerage, storage, and exam charges are not reflected in your product economics, your Shopify margin report will look healthier than the business really is.

Step 7: Confirm records access and retention

Recordkeeping is not a nice-to-have. CBP has separate recordkeeping requirements in 19 CFR Part 163, and CBP guidance says entry records generally must be kept for five years from the date of entry or the activity that required the record to be created. CBSA's importing guidance also tells importers using a broker that they remain responsible for accounting documentation and subsequent corrections.

Ask the broker:

  • What documents will you provide after each entry?
  • Can we download entry packets from a portal?
  • How long do you retain broker records?
  • How quickly can you retrieve a past entry packet?
  • What happens if we change brokers?
  • How do you handle confidential client records?
  • What is your process for electronic records and backup?

Do not let your customs records live only in someone's email inbox. Store entry summaries, commercial invoices, packing lists, bills of lading or airway bills, product specs, classification support, origin support, duty payment evidence, and broker correspondence in a shared company system.

Step 8: Ask about error handling before there is an error

The best time to discuss corrections is before a correction is needed.

Ask:

  • How do you handle a wrong HTS code discovered after entry?
  • How do you handle a supplier invoice error?
  • Who identifies whether a post-entry correction, prior disclosure, refund, protest, or Canadian adjustment is appropriate?
  • Do you alert clients when tariff actions change duty exposure?
  • Do you carry errors and omissions coverage?
  • What types of mistakes are excluded by your service agreement?

A broker should not promise that every issue can be fixed painlessly. They should be able to explain the process, the decision points, and when trade counsel may be needed.

Step 9: Connect broker data to Shopify operations

This is the part many traditional broker checklists miss.

For Shopify merchants, customs data should not sit outside the commerce system. HS codes, country of origin, item cost, duty assumptions, and fulfillment method can affect pricing, duties at checkout, product profitability, and customer delivery promises.

At minimum:

  • Add HS codes to Shopify products where your setup uses them; Shopify's Admin API docs expose country of origin and country-specific HS-code fields[4] on inventory items, which is a useful reminder that customs data belongs in product operations, not only in broker email threads.
  • Track country of origin at SKU level, especially if the same product can come from different factories.
  • Keep supplier commercial invoices aligned with product descriptions your broker uses.
  • Review duty rates before buying inventory, not only after the broker files entry.
  • Recheck landed cost when changing supplier, material, bundle composition, or destination market.
  • Decide whether duties are charged to customers, absorbed in margin, or handled through DDP terms.

If you collect duties at checkout or advertise landed-cost certainty, your customs data needs tighter controls. A wrong HS code is not only a broker problem; it can become a pricing, refund, and customer trust problem.

Red flags when vetting a broker

Walk away or slow down if you see these:

  • They will not give you a license name or license number.
  • They say classification is always "whatever the supplier puts on the invoice."
  • They dismiss country of origin as the same thing as ship-from country.
  • They will not identify who files the entry.
  • They ask you to sign a POA without naming the broker entity.
  • They cannot explain how customs notices are handled.
  • They avoid written fees.
  • They do not provide entry documents after clearance.
  • They treat Shopify product data as irrelevant.
  • They promise a duty rate without reviewing product facts.

The broker does not need to be perfect. They need to be transparent, licensed, responsive, and disciplined about facts.

Broker onboarding template

Use this message when approaching a broker:

We run a Shopify store importing [product category] from [origin countries] into [destination country]. We are looking for a licensed customs broker to support classification review, entry filing, record access, and customs issue handling. Before signing a POA, can you confirm your license details, experience with this category, fee schedule, onboarding document checklist, entry packet access, and escalation process for holds or customs notices?

Attach:

  • A sample commercial invoice.
  • Product photos or spec sheets.
  • SKU list with materials, use, and origin.
  • Expected shipment mode and volume.
  • Destination market and port or courier.
  • Current HS or HTS codes, if you have them.
  • Any duties-at-checkout or DDP process you use.

That one package will filter serious brokers from casual ones quickly.

FAQ

Do Shopify merchants legally need a customs broker?

Not always. An importer can often transact customs business for its own account, and couriers may provide brokerage on small parcel shipments. A licensed customs broker becomes much more important when you import formal entries, ocean freight, regulated goods, multi-line shipments, or products where classification, origin, or valuation is uncertain. In the United States, 19 CFR Part 111[2] regulates customs brokers; in Canada, CBSA says importers may prepare their own release/accounting documents or authorize a licensed customs broker[5].

Does using a broker remove importer liability?

No. A broker can file and coordinate customs activity on your behalf, but importers still have responsibilities. CBP's reasonable care[1] framework and CBSA's importing guidance[5] both make clear that importers should not treat broker use as a substitute for accurate information and recordkeeping.

Should I use the broker recommended by my freight forwarder?

Maybe, but verify the broker separately. Ask whether the forwarder is itself licensed, uses an affiliated broker, or subcontracts to a third party. Get the broker's legal name, license details, POA process, and fee schedule before allowing them to file entries for your business.

What documents should I give a broker for a new product?

Provide the commercial invoice, packing list, product photos, material composition, intended use, supplier details, country of origin, purchase order, shipping documents, and any current HS or HTS code. For regulated products, include test reports, certificates, labels, ingredients, or technical documentation as relevant.

How often should I review my broker relationship?

Review it at least annually, and sooner if you change suppliers, product materials, destination markets, shipment modes, or duties-at-checkout strategy. Also review after any customs hold, exam, rejected entry, surprise duty bill, or repeated communication problem.

What is the biggest customs broker red flag?

The biggest red flag is vague accountability. If no one can clearly say who is the licensed broker, who signs the POA, who files the entry, who checks the classification, and who gives you the entry packet, the process is too loose for a growing Shopify import operation.

Can a broker help me reduce duties?

A broker can help identify correct classifications, duty preference opportunities, documentation gaps, and special-duty exposure. They should not promise artificial duty reduction. Legal duty planning depends on accurate classification, valuation, origin, sourcing, and trade-program eligibility.

Do I still need a landed-cost tool if I have a broker?

Yes, if you make buying or pricing decisions before goods ship. A broker helps with compliance and entry filing. A landed-cost tool helps you estimate duties and margin impact earlier, while you still have time to change price, supplier, shipping terms, or purchase quantity.
