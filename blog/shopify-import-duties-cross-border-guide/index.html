How Tariffs Work on Shopify

When a customer in the US purchases from your Shopify store and receives goods from outside the US, import duties apply. These duties are calculated based on three factors:

  • Product classification (HTS code)
  • Country of origin
  • Declared value of the goods

Shopify doesn't automatically calculate duties—that's your responsibility. However, Shopify offers tools to help you disclose duties to customers during checkout, which is critical for transparency and customer satisfaction.

⚠️ Important: As of February 2026, the US Supreme Court invalidated several emergency tariff programs that were temporarily in place. Current rates reflect base tariff schedules plus any permanent trade programs (Section 301, Section 232, etc.). Always verify current rates against the USITC HTS database before publishing prices.

De Minimis Threshold: What Changed in 2026?

This is the biggest change affecting cross-border sellers in 2026. The US de minimis exemption has been suspended.

What was de minimis? Previously, shipments valued under $800 entered the US duty-free and required minimal customs documentation. This made it incredibly cheap and easy for international sellers to send small packages without customs paperwork.

What changed? Effective February 24, 2026, all imports, regardless of value, are now subject to duties and formal customs entry. This means:

  • Even a $5 item from China now triggers duty collection
  • Customs brokers are required for all entries
  • Formal entry paperwork is mandatory
  • Processing times have increased

For Shopify merchants, this fundamentally changes the economics of cross-border selling. Small orders that were previously cost-effective to ship are now burdened with brokerage fees ($15-40 per shipment).

💡 Smart move: Consider increasing minimum order values or offering fulfillment from a warehouse in the destination country to avoid the de minimis elimination entirely. Some merchants are shifting from B2C to B2B models to maintain margins.

DDP vs DDU: Which Pricing Model Should You Use?

These are two different ways to handle duties in your Shopify checkout:

DDP: Delivered Duty Paid

You (the seller) collect duties from the customer at checkout and pay them to customs on behalf of the customer. You're the "importer of record."

Pros:

  • No surprise duties at delivery—customer sees total price upfront
  • Better conversion rates (no sticker shock at door)
  • Smoother customer experience

Cons:

  • You absorb duty payment risk
  • You must estimate duties accurately (overestimate = lost margin, underestimate = customer refund complexity)
  • Requires DDP-capable fulfillment partner or in-house processing
  • More complex accounting

DDU: Delivered Duty Unpaid

The customer pays duties directly to customs/the courier at delivery. The customer becomes the "importer of record."

Pros:

  • No duty payment risk on your side
  • Simpler logistics
  • Customer controls duty payment timing

Cons:

  • Customer sees duty charges at delivery (surprises = returns)
  • Lower conversion rates for cross-border orders
  • Customer friction increases
  • Courier holds package until duty paid

Recommendation for Shopify merchants: If you're in a competitive market, DDP wins on conversion. If you have low-volume or high-margin products, DDU keeps things simple. Many successful cross-border merchants use DDP for consumer orders and DDU for bulk/B2B orders.

Finding the Right HTS Code for Your Products

The Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) is a standardized classification system used globally. Every physical good has an HTS code that determines the applicable tariff rate.

Why it matters: A small misclassification can swing duties by 10-40%. A "metal clip" (non-dutiable) vs "industrial fastener" (12% tariff) looks similar but costs very differently.

How to Find Your HTS Code

  1. Use the USITC Database: Go to hts.usitc.gov, search your product description (e.g., "ceramic mug," "cotton t-shirt"), and find the matching code.
  2. Check existing shipments: If you've imported before, your customs broker's paperwork will show the HTS code used.
  3. Hire a customs broker: For ambiguous products, a broker can obtain a binding classification from CBP (about $200-500 per ruling).
  4. Use TariffShield's calculator: Our duty calculator helps you estimate rates once you know your HTS code and country of origin.

Note: HTS codes are updated annually (January 1st). Check dataweb.usitc.gov for 2026 rates, which are current through March 2, 2026.

Calculating Landed Costs: The Complete Formula

Landed cost = Product cost + Shipping + Tariffs + Brokerage + Handling

Let's walk through a real example:

Scenario: You're importing ceramic mugs from Vietnam to sell on Shopify.

  • Product cost per unit: $2.50
  • Air freight to US (per unit): $0.80
  • Country of origin: Vietnam
  • HTS Code: 6912.00 (Ceramic tableware)
  • Tariff rate from Vietnam: 0% (USMCA benefit, Vietnam has no FTA with US—actually 2.5% base rate)
  • Shipment value: $500 (200 units × $2.50)
  • Customs brokerage fee: $35

Calculation:

  • Duty = $500 × 2.5% = $12.50
  • Landed cost per unit = ($2.50 + $0.80 + $12.50÷200 + $35÷200) = $3.70

If you're selling at $9.99 and Shopify takes 3%, your gross margin is 72%—healthy. But if you miscalculate the tariff rate, your margin evaporates.

Legal Compliance & Documentation

When goods cross borders, documentation is everything. CBP (US Customs and Border Protection) requires:

  • Commercial invoice: Itemized breakdown of goods, prices, and terms of sale
  • Packing list: Which items are in which boxes (aids inspection)
  • Bill of lading or air waybill: Proof of shipment
  • Country of origin marking: Each product must be marked with "Made in [Country]"
  • Importer of record (IOR) information: Your ITIN or EIN, plus full address

If you're using DDP, your fulfillment partner or customs broker handles this. If DDU, the responsibility shifts to the customer (though it's good practice to provide documentation).

Common Compliance Mistakes to Avoid

  • Undervaluing goods: Declaring a $100 item as $50 to save on duties is customs fraud. CBP cross-checks invoice prices against market rates.
  • Wrong country of origin: If 60% of the value is added in an FTA country (Canada/Mexico), you might qualify for USMCA rates. Wrong origin = overpaid duties or denied entry.
  • Missing tariff classification: Don't guess. A wrong HTS code can cause delays or duty recalculation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to collect sales tax on cross-border orders?
Sales tax applies differently by state and destination country. Generally, if the customer is outside the US, no US sales tax applies. However, the destination country may impose VAT (Europe) or GST (Canada). For US orders, you're liable for sales tax in states where you have "nexus." Shopify can help calculate this in checkout settings.
Can I avoid duties by shipping from a third country?
Not legally. The origin of goods is determined by where they're manufactured, not where they're shipped from. CBP looks at the commercial invoice to determine origin. However, assembly in Mexico or Canada under USMCA can qualify for 0% duties if rules of origin are met.
What's the difference between a customs broker and a freight forwarder?
A customs broker is licensed by CBP to clear goods through customs, file paperwork, and handle duty payments. A freight forwarder arranges transportation and logistics but isn't licensed to handle customs clearance. You need both for full international shipping: forwarder handles transport, broker handles customs.
How long does customs clearance take now that de minimis is gone?
Without de minimis, all packages require formal entry. Typical clearance is 3-7 business days, but it can extend to 2+ weeks during peak periods (holiday season). Plan for longer transit times when calculating delivery expectations for customers.
If I collect duties from the customer (DDP), can I refund if customs reduces the duties?
Yes. If you estimate duties at $50 but CBP assesses $30, you're required to refund the difference. This is why accurate estimation is crucial. Many merchants build in a 10-15% contingency to avoid refunds. Use historical CBP entry data to improve accuracy over time.
Are there any free trade agreements that reduce my tariffs?
Yes. The main ones affecting Shopify merchants are USMCA (Canada/Mexico—0% for qualifying goods), GSP (certain developing countries—reduced rates), and various FTAs (Korea, Australia, etc.). Our CUSMA/USMCA guide covers this in detail. If your product qualifies, savings can be substantial.

Key Takeaways

  • De minimis is dead. All imports now face duties and brokerage costs, changing the economics of small international orders.
  • DDP > DDU for conversion. Collecting duties upfront removes surprise charges and improves customer experience.
  • HTS code = everything. Misclassify and your margins evaporate. Use official databases to get it right.
  • Landed cost is more than product + shipping. Add duties, brokerage, handling, and currency fluctuation to your pricing model.
  • Document meticulously. CBP audits cross-border commerce aggressively. Proper invoicing and origin documentation protects you.
  • Monitor tariff changes. Rates update January 1 each year and can change mid-year for specific countries/products. Check USITC DataWeb quarterly.
