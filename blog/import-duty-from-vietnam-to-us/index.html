Current US Tariff Rates on Vietnamese Goods (March 2026)

Vietnam is not subject to Section 301 tariffs — that regime targets China specifically. For most Vietnamese goods, you're paying two layers of duty:

Source: USITC Harmonized Tariff Schedule; Tax Foundation Tariff Tracker, March 2026.

✅ Bottom line: For most consumer goods from Vietnam — apparel, footwear, furniture, electronics — your effective duty rate in March 2026 is approximately 10–15% (Section 122 + HTS base rate). That's significantly lower than the 17.5–35% faced by comparable Chinese goods.

The Section 122 baseline (10%) is temporary and set to expire around July 23, 2026. If it expires, Vietnamese goods with a 0% HTS rate would pay zero duty — the same as pre-tariff-war levels.

Why Apparel Rates Are So High

You'll notice that apparel and footwear have high HTS base rates — these are pre-existing US tariffs that pre-date the current trade war by decades. The US textile and footwear industries have enjoyed tariff protection since the 1970s. Vietnam doesn't get a free trade agreement with the US (unlike Canada and Mexico under USMCA), so these base rates apply in full.

For some specialized clothing categories, HTS base rates can exceed 30%, meaning the total effective duty could reach 40%+ even from Vietnam.

How to Calculate Your Landed Cost from Vietnam

Your total landed cost = Product cost + Shipping + Insurance + Duty + Customs broker fee

Compare that to the same order from China, where furniture faces Section 301 (25%) on top — you'd pay roughly $13,600 more in duty on this same $40,000 order. Over a year of purchasing, Vietnam sourcing can save tens of thousands in tariffs alone.

💡 Tip: Duty is calculated on CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight), not just the product cost. Shipping costs inflate your dutiable value, so optimizing your freight can reduce your duty bill. A $500 savings on freight saves you $65 in duty (at 13%).

The Transshipment Problem: What US Customs Watches For

As more goods are routed through Vietnam to avoid China tariffs, US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) has intensified scrutiny of transshipment — goods that originate in China but pass through Vietnam to falsely claim a lower-tariff origin.

CBP uses several methods to catch transshipment:

  • Country of Origin verification: CBP can demand full production records, not just the last manufacturing step
  • Substantial transformation test: Goods must be meaningfully transformed in Vietnam — just re-labeling, minor assembly, or simple cutting/sewing of Chinese-made fabric doesn't qualify
  • Audit programs: CBP runs targeted enforcement programs against high-risk product categories like furniture, apparel, and solar panels
  • Fines and seizures: Transshipment can result in seizure of goods, back-duties, and civil penalties up to 4× the unpaid duty

If you're working with a Vietnam-based supplier who sources raw materials or semi-finished goods from China, document the production process carefully. The rule of thumb: if your goods are substantially transformed in Vietnam (cut fabric that becomes a finished garment, raw components assembled into a functional product), you're likely fine. If Vietnam is just a transit point or minimal processing step, you're at risk.

⚠ Due diligence matters: If you suspect your supplier is transshipping, the legal liability falls on the importer of record — you. Request factory certifications, ask about raw material sourcing, and consider a third-party factory audit.

The invalidation of IEEPA was actually a significant win for Vietnamese goods specifically. The proposed 46% reciprocal tariff would have dramatically cut Vietnam's competitiveness. Now, Vietnam faces only the 10% Section 122 plus standard HTS rates — a much more manageable situation.

The US-Vietnam Trade Deficit

The US-Vietnam goods trade deficit reached $178.2 billion in 2025 — a 44% jump from 2024 — driven by the sourcing shift away from China. This deficit is a major reason Vietnam was targeted by the IEEPA reciprocal tariff regime. Whether future administrations renew trade pressure on Vietnam remains a key risk to watch.

According to USTR data, total US goods imports from Vietnam were $193.8 billion in 2025, making Vietnam the second-largest import partner after China in several product categories.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the import duty rate from Vietnam to the US in 2026?
Most Vietnamese goods face a 10% baseline tariff (Section 122) plus the standard HTS duty rate (typically 0–15% depending on the product). Unlike China, Vietnam is not subject to Section 301 tariffs. Total effective duty on most goods is typically 10–25%, significantly lower than Chinese imports. Exception: steel and aluminum products face Section 232 tariffs (50%) regardless of origin.
Q: Is Vietnam subject to Section 301 tariffs?
No. Section 301 tariffs are China-specific and do not apply to goods that genuinely originate in Vietnam. However, US Customs actively investigates transshipment — goods manufactured in China but shipped through Vietnam to avoid China tariffs. The importer of record bears legal responsibility for accurate country-of-origin claims.
Q: How does importing from Vietnam compare to importing from China?
Vietnam is significantly cheaper on tariffs for most products. A typical consumer good from China faces 17.5–35% in combined duties. The same good from Vietnam typically faces 10–15%. Furniture from China can face 35–40% duty, versus 10–15% from Vietnam. However, Vietnam's base manufacturing cost is often somewhat higher than China's, so the total landed cost comparison varies by product.
Q: What did the US-Vietnam trade deal in 2025 change?
The US negotiated a bilateral framework with Vietnam under the IEEPA reciprocal tariff regime, agreeing to ~20%. With IEEPA struck down by the Supreme Court on February 20, 2026, that deal effectively became moot. Current applicable rates reverted to standard HTS rates plus the 10% Section 122 baseline — a better outcome for importers than the negotiated 20%.
Q: Does the Section 122 tariff apply to Vietnam?
Yes. The 10% Section 122 baseline tariff enacted on February 24, 2026 applies to imports from virtually all countries, including Vietnam. It is set to expire after 150 days (around July 23, 2026) unless extended by the administration or replaced with new authority.
Q: If I'm sourcing from Vietnam to avoid China tariffs, what documentation should I keep?
Keep full production records showing substantial transformation occurred in Vietnam: raw material sourcing invoices, factory records, bills of materials, production photos or video, and a formal Certificate of Origin (CO) issued by the Vietnam Chamber of Commerce and Industry (VCCI). The CO alone is not sufficient — CBP can and does look beyond it during audits.
