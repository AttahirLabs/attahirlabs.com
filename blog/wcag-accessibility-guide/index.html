WCAG Accessibility for Ecommerce: Why Overlay Widgets Don't Work and What Actually Does

If you own a Shopify store or ecommerce site, you've probably seen ads for accessibility overlays: "Just add our widget and you're ADA compliant." It's tempting. It's also a trap — and courts have started saying so. Here's what you actually need to know about WCAG compliance, why overlays fail, and what genuinely protects your business.

Why Accessibility Matters NOW (2026)

Three things changed in 2025-2026 that make this urgent:

1. ADA Lawsuits Hit Record Highs

4,605 ADA Title III lawsuits were filed in 2024 — the highest year on record, according to the UsableNet annual report. Over 800 of those businesses were specifically sued despite using accessibility overlay widgets. Courts are beginning to recognize that overlays alone don't satisfy legal requirements.

The pattern: a plaintiff's attorney finds a business with an accessibility overlay, tests it with a screen reader, finds accessibility barriers, and sues. The overlay is irrelevant if the underlying HTML is broken.

2. The European Accessibility Act (EAA) Deadline

If you sell to EU customers, mark your calendar: June 28, 2025 is when the European Accessibility Act takes effect. It requires all online retailers selling physical goods or services in the EU to meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards. This applies to your Shopify store even if you're based in North America.

The EAA covers the same web accessibility standards as the US ADA (WCAG 2.1 AA). No overlays. Real fixes.

3. Canada's AODA Keeps Growing

The Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) has required Ontario businesses to meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards since 2021. Other Canadian provinces are following suit. For Ontario merchants, this is law.

The moral case matters too: 1 in 5 people in North America have a disability. If your site is hard to use for them, you're literally turning away customers and violating their rights.

What is WCAG 2.1 Level AA?

WCAG stands for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. Level AA is the middle-ground standard that regulators actually enforce. Here's what it means:

The 4 Principles (POUR)

PrincipleWhat It MeansExample
PerceivableContent must be visible/hearable for all usersAlt text on images; captions on videos; sufficient color contrast
OperableUsers must be able to navigate and use all featuresFull keyboard navigation; buttons that work without a mouse
UnderstandableInformation and instructions must be clearPlain language; consistent navigation; labeled form fields
RobustCode must work with assistive technologiesProper HTML semantics; ARIA labels where needed

WCAG 2.1 Level AA has 50 specific criteria across these four principles. You don't need to pass every single one to be "compliant" — but courts and regulators expect you to make a good-faith effort and fix violations when you find them.

Top 5 Accessibility Violations on Ecommerce Sites

Based on WebAIM's annual analysis and WCAG compliance reports, these are the five violations that appear in 99% of inaccessible ecommerce sites:

1. Missing Alt Text on Product Images (The #1 Violation)

What it is: Images without descriptive alt text. A blind user with a screen reader hears nothing; they don't know what the product looks like.

How to fix it:

  • Write descriptive alt text for every product image: <img src="shoe.jpg" alt="Red Nike Air Force 1 Low, size 10, leather">
  • Don't just write "shoe" or "product image" — describe the key details
  • For Shopify: Use the "Alt Text" field in your product image settings (takes 30 seconds per image)
  • SEO bonus: Google uses alt text for image search ranking. Better alt text = more traffic from Google Images

Real impact: A store with 1,000 products and no alt text has 1,000 accessibility violations. Fix this alone and your accessibility score jumps 40%.

2. Low Color Contrast (Text Hard to Read)

What it is: Light gray text on white, or dark gray on black. Hard for people with low vision, and fails WCAG AA standards.

How to fix it:

  • Test your colors: Use the free TPGI Contrast Checker
  • Minimum contrast ratio: 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text
  • Dark text on light background (or vice versa) is almost always safe
  • Avoid: light gray (#aaa or lighter), especially for body text

Real impact: Better contrast also improves readability for everyone on mobile devices in sunlight.

3. Missing Form Labels and ARIA

What it is: Form fields without labels, or labels not properly connected to inputs. A screen reader user doesn't know what each field is for.

How to fix it:

  • Every input needs a <label> tag: <label for="email">Email Address</label><input id="email" type="email">
  • Use ARIA-label for buttons without text: <button aria-label="Close menu">✕</button>
  • For Shopify forms: Most built-in forms are fine, but custom forms need checking

4. Non-Semantic HTML (Wrong HTML Tags)

What it is: Using <div> for buttons, <span> for headings, or <div> for links. Screen readers don't understand what these elements do.

How to fix it:

  • Use semantic HTML: <button> for buttons (not <div onclick>)
  • Use <nav> for navigation, <main> for main content, <article> for articles
  • Use real <a href> links, not <span> with click handlers
  • Use proper heading hierarchy: <h1> once per page, then <h2>, <h3>, etc.

5. No Keyboard Navigation (Mouse-Only)

What it is: Important features only work with a mouse. A keyboard user or screen reader user can't access them.

How to fix it:

  • Test: Open your site and navigate using only Tab, Shift+Tab, Enter, and arrow keys. Can you add items to cart? Checkout? Complete forms?
  • If you get stuck, something isn't keyboard-accessible
  • All interactive elements need focus styles so users know where they are
  • Most Shopify themes handle this automatically, but custom code often breaks it

Why Overlay Widgets Fail (And What Courts Say)

Accessibility overlay companies have raised hundreds of millions in venture capital. They all make the same promise: "Add our widget, become ADA compliant instantly." It sounds great. It's also not how accessibility works.

How Overlays Actually Work

An accessibility overlay injects JavaScript into your page at runtime. It attempts to:

  • Increase text size automatically
  • Adjust color contrast
  • Add "missing" alt text to images
  • Simulate keyboard navigation

The problem: Overlays can't fix broken HTML. They can't make a non-semantic <div> button actually work for screen readers. They can't retroactively add proper labels to forms. They can't replace missing alt text in a way that a screen reader understands (they add it visually, but not to the actual DOM).

What Courts Are Saying

The landmark case: In Lainey v. Uber (Northern District of California, 2024), a blind user sued Uber because its accessibility overlay failed to make the website usable. The court found that the overlay was no substitute for building accessible code.

The pattern: Multiple court filings reference that overlays are "band-aids on fundamental accessibility problems." Even if you install an overlay, if your underlying HTML is broken, you're still liable.

National Federation of the Blind (NFB) Statement: The NFB, the largest disability rights organization in the US, has issued formal statements opposing accessibility overlays, saying they don't meaningfully improve access for screen reader users.

Sources: Lainey v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2024), National Federation of the Blind statement on accessibility overlays (2023).

⚠ The Real Risk: If you have an overlay, a plaintiff's attorney sees this: "They know they have accessibility problems (why else buy an overlay?) but didn't fix them." This can make your legal position worse, not better. Overlays can be evidence of negligence, not compliance.

What Actually Works: Real Accessibility Fixes

Courts, regulators, and the disability community all agree: actual accessibility fixes work. Here's what you need to do:

1. Add Alt Text to All Images

This is the single highest-impact fix you can make. Most ecommerce sites have 500+ images with zero alt text.

  • Shopify: Edit each product → Image → Alt text field. Takes ~2 minutes per product for a thorough description
  • AI shortcut: Tools like Alt Text Generator or Siteimprove can auto-generate alt text, but review them — they're not perfect
  • What to write: Describe the product as if you were describing it to someone who can't see the image. Include color, material, key features

2. Use Proper HTML Semantics

If you built your site with Shopify's standard themes, this is mostly handled for you. If you have custom code:

  • Use <button> for buttons, not <div>
  • Use <a href=""> for links
  • Use <nav>, <main>, <article>, <section> tags appropriately
  • Use <label> for form fields

3. Add ARIA Labels Where Needed

ARIA = Accessible Rich Internet Applications. It's a set of HTML attributes that tell screen readers what things are.

  • aria-label: Label for an element with no visible text. Example: <button aria-label="Close menu">✕</button>
  • aria-labelledby: Links an element to another element that labels it
  • role: Tells screen readers what an element does. Example: <div role="button" tabindex="0"> (but just use <button> instead)

4. Ensure Full Keyboard Navigation

Every interactive element must work with Tab, Enter, Escape, and arrow keys:

  • All buttons, links, and form fields must be reachable via Tab
  • Focus styles must be visible (don't remove the blue outline!)
  • Dropdown menus must work with arrow keys
  • Modals must trap focus and allow escape to close

5. Fix Color Contrast

  • Test your colors: TPGI Contrast Checker
  • Minimum 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text (18pt+ or 14pt+ bold)
  • Avoid light gray text; dark text on light backgrounds is safest

6. Provide Captions and Transcripts for Videos

  • If you have product demo videos, add captions (YouTube auto-generates them, but review for accuracy)
  • Provide transcripts for any video with spoken content

How to Audit Your Site for Free

You don't need to hire a consultant. Start with these free tools:

1. Google Lighthouse (Built Into Chrome)

How to use:

  1. Open your Shopify store in Chrome
  2. Right-click → Inspect (or Cmd+Option+I on Mac)
  3. Go to the "Lighthouse" tab
  4. Click "Analyze page load" and select "Accessibility"
  5. Wait 30 seconds — you'll get a score (0-100) and a list of violations

Lighthouse is built-in, free, and gives you real, specific fixes. Don't use it as your only test, but it's a great starting point.

2. WAVE by WebAIM

How to use:

  1. Go to wave.webaim.org
  2. Paste your site URL
  3. It highlights accessibility errors directly on your page in red

Great for visual learners. Shows errors, warnings, and features in context.

3. axe DevTools (Chrome Extension)

How to use:

  1. Install the free axe DevTools extension
  2. Right-click → Inspect
  3. Go to the "axe DevTools" tab
  4. Click "Scan ALL of my page" — it finds violations and explains how to fix them

axe is used by accessibility professionals. It's thorough and has helpful explanations.

4. Manual Screen Reader Testing

Tools are helpful, but real screen reader testing is the gold standard:

  • Mac: Press Cmd+F5 to enable VoiceOver (built-in)
  • Windows: NVDA is free (download at nvaccess.org)
  • Use it to navigate your site: Can you add products to cart? Complete checkout? Fill out a contact form?
  • If you get confused, your users will too

Pro tip: Accessibility isn't just about screen readers. Test your site with:

  • Keyboard only (no mouse)
  • Browser zoom at 200%
  • Text size increased to 125-150%
  • Color contrast filters (simulate color blindness)

The Business Case for Accessibility

Accessibility isn't just about legal compliance. It's good business:

1. Legal Protection (ADA, EAA, AODA Compliance)

  • 4,605 ADA lawsuits in 2024 — you don't want to be one
  • EAA applies to all EU retail starting June 2025
  • AODA is law in Ontario (and growing in Canada)
  • Real fixes are your defense; overlays are not

2. SEO Improvement

  • Alt text: Google uses it for image search ranking. Better alt = more traffic from Google Images
  • Semantic HTML: Helps Google understand your content structure
  • Keyboard navigation: Makes your site easier to crawl
  • Page speed: Accessibility-friendly code is often faster code

3. Expanded Customer Base

  • 1 in 5 people have a disability
  • Accessibility helps: blind users, low-vision users, deaf users, motor-disabled users, cognitively disabled users
  • It also helps: elderly users, people on low-bandwidth connections, people using mobile devices in sunlight
  • More accessible = more customers

4. Brand Reputation

  • Customers notice accessibility (or lack thereof)
  • Accessibility is part of modern design and brand values
  • Lawsuits are public and damaging to reputation

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do I need to be 100% compliant with WCAG 2.1 AA?
No. The standard is "reasonable accessibility," not perfection. Courts and regulators expect you to make good-faith effort and fix violations when you find them. Get a Lighthouse score above 85-90, pass major automated audits, and test with real users. If you're at 95%+ compliance and someone finds a bug, you have a strong legal position.
Q: Are accessibility overlay widgets ever useful?
Not for compliance. Overlays can occasionally help some users with font resizing or color adjustment, but courts have ruled they don't satisfy legal requirements. The National Federation of the Blind opposes them. Save your money and fix your actual HTML instead.
Q: Can I use an accessibility service to fix my site?
Yes — but choose carefully. Look for services that actually fix your code (modify HTML, add alt text, improve semantics), not services that just add overlays. Many accessibility agencies will audit your site and provide a roadmap for fixes. Budget $1,000-$5,000 for a thorough review and initial fixes on a typical ecommerce site.
Q: I'm on Shopify. Do I need to fix my theme?
Most modern Shopify themes are reasonably accessible out of the box. Focus on: (1) adding alt text to product images, (2) checking color contrast in your custom CSS, (3) testing keyboard navigation. If you've customized code heavily, have someone audit it. Default Shopify features are usually fine.
Q: What happens if I get sued for accessibility violations?
If a plaintiff sues under the ADA, they're typically seeking damages + attorney fees. Settlements range from $5,000 to $50,000+, depending on severity and how long you were aware of the issue. Courts are less sympathetic if you had an overlay but never fixed the underlying code. Prevention is much cheaper than litigation.
Q: Does the EAA actually apply to me if I'm in North America?
If you sell to anyone in the EU (even if you don't ship there), the EAA applies to you. Same standard as ADA: WCAG 2.1 AA. Check your Shopify analytics — if you have any EU traffic, you're in scope.
Q: I've hired an accessibility firm — what should I look for in a report?
Good accessibility reports include: (1) specific violations with examples, (2) how each violation affects users, (3) prioritized fix roadmap (quick wins first), (4) estimated effort for each fix, (5) re-audit plan. If a report just says "you're not compliant" without actionable fixes, get a second opinion.
Q: Can I use AI to write alt text for all my product images?
Yes, with caveats. Tools like GPT-4 Vision or Google's image analysis can generate decent alt text. But review them — AI sometimes misses details or hallucinates. For critical product images, write or edit the alt text yourself. For decorative images, AI is fine. Mix of both approaches saves time while maintaining quality.
