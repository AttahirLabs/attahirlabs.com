De Minimis Threshold Changes 2026: What Dropshippers and Small Importers Need to Know

The $800 rule is dead. Executive Order 14324 suspended duty-free de minimis treatment for all countries — meaning every small package from overseas now owes duties at the border. If your business model depends on cheap, untaxed small shipments from AliExpress, Temu, or any overseas supplier, this changes everything.

🚨 This is not a drill: US Customs and Border Protection has already issued operational guidance (CSMS #66311990) telling carriers how to collect duties on international mail shipments. The exemption is gone. CBP is collecting duties now.

What Was the De Minimis Exemption? (The $800 Rule)

For years, US law had a simple, powerful rule: if a package's value was under $800, it entered the United States completely duty-free. No tariffs. No customs forms beyond a basic postal declaration. No broker fees. Just arrive and go.

This was codified in 19 U.S.C. § 1321, known as the "de minimis" provision — Latin for "about minimal things." The $800 threshold was set in 2015 and was remarkably high by global standards. For comparison, Canada's threshold is CAD $20, and the EU is €150.

The $800 threshold created an enormous opportunity. It meant:

  • A consumer ordering a $50 phone case from AliExpress paid zero duty
  • A dropshipper shipping individual items directly from China to US customers paid zero duty per shipment
  • Temu and Shein could ship individual orders directly to US buyers, bypassing the 7.5–25% tariffs that applied to bulk importers
  • Small importers testing products could bring in samples without worrying about customs complications

The de minimis exemption was processing hundreds of millions of packages per year. By 2023, CBP reported that de minimis shipments accounted for over 1 billion packages annually — roughly 2.7 million per day. The volume had grown 600% over a decade, driven almost entirely by e-commerce from China.

💡 Why did this matter so much for Chinese sellers? Under de minimis, a Temu or Shein package shipped directly from China to a US consumer owed no duty. But if a US-based importer brought in the same item in bulk, they'd pay 7.5–25% in tariffs. This gave Chinese direct-to-consumer sellers a massive structural cost advantage over traditional importers.

What Changed in 2026 — Executive Order 14324

On February 1, 2025, President Trump signed an executive order targeting de minimis — initially focused on China-origin shipments. But the rules evolved rapidly through 2025. By early 2026, the administration took the most sweeping step yet:

Executive Order 14324 suspended duty-free de minimis treatment for ALL countries. Not just China. Not just certain carriers. All international small packages lost their automatic duty-free pass.

Following the executive order, CBP issued CSMS #66311990 — official guidance to postal operators and carriers on how to collect duties on international mail. This is the operational instruction that moved de minimis closure from policy to reality at the border.

The Tax Foundation estimates that eliminating de minimis will raise approximately $72.9 billion in federal revenue over the 2026–2035 period (conventional estimate). That's how much money was flowing through duty-free — and that's now being captured as tariff revenue.

Who's Affected: Dropshippers, AliExpress Buyers, Small Importers

The de minimis closure hits a wide swath of the e-commerce ecosystem. Here's who's getting hurt:

Dropshippers Shipping Direct from China

Classic dropshipping — list a product on Shopify, buy it from AliExpress, ship it directly to the customer — relied almost entirely on de minimis. Every order was under $800. Every order went duty-free. That's over. Each customer shipment now owes duty based on the product value and applicable tariff rate. For a $40 item from China, that could mean $10–15 in duties, which the seller either absorbs (killing margins) or passes to the customer (killing conversions).

AliExpress and Temu Buyers

Individual consumers ordering from AliExpress, Temu, or Shein are now affected too. That $35 phone case, $22 LED strip light, or $60 clothing order now crosses the border with duty owed. Many will see this as unexpected customs charges on delivery, or platforms will pre-collect the duty at checkout. This is a significant change in the economics of buying from these platforms.

Temu had already paused US advertising on Meta (Facebook/Instagram) in 2025, partly due to the combination of de minimis changes and China tariffs making their cost structure untenable for the US market. The full suspension of de minimis makes that calculation even harder.

DTC Brands Sourcing from China

Direct-to-consumer brands that manufacture in China and ship small batches to customers around the world used de minimis as a critical margin buffer. Orders under $800 that previously cleared customs automatically now generate duty bills that cut directly into gross margin.

Small Business Importers and Product Testers

Small businesses that import samples, test products, or buy in small quantities for resale are affected. Where previously a 10-unit test order at $50/unit ($500 total) would clear duty-free, it now generates a customs bill before you've proven the product will sell.

⚠ The hidden cost: compliance burden. It's not just the duty itself. De minimis shipments bypassed detailed customs entry requirements. Now, carriers must submit proper entry data for every package. This generates brokerage fees, processing delays, and administrative overhead — even if the duty amount is small.

How Duties Now Work on Small Packages

Without de minimis protection, every international package must now pay the appropriate duty for its contents. Here's how the process works:

Duty Is Based on Product Value + Country of Origin

Your duty is calculated as a percentage of the shipment's value. The rate depends on:

  1. Country of origin — where the goods were made (not just shipped from)
  2. HTS code — the product classification code that determines the tariff rate
  3. Declared value — what you (or the seller) declare the goods are worth

For China-Origin Goods

This is where it gets brutal. Chinese goods no longer have a $800 free pass AND they face elevated tariff rates. A small package from China now owes:

  • Section 301 tariff: 7.5–25% depending on product category
  • Section 122 tariff: 10% (currently active)
  • MFN (base) tariff rate: Typically 0–12% for most consumer goods

Combined, most consumer goods from China face 17.5–35% in duties. For electronics and apparel, the Section 301 rate is 7.5%, so a typical consumer electronics package might face 17.5–20% in total duties.

For Non-China Goods

Packages from countries like Vietnam, India, Turkey, and Mexico now face their applicable MFN tariff rate plus the Section 122 10% baseline. For many consumer goods, MFN rates are low (0–5%), so a Vietnam-origin package might face only 10–15% in duties. Still a cost that didn't exist before, but less severe than China.

Who Collects the Duty

CBP has issued guidance (CSMS #66311990) to postal operators and express carriers on how to collect duties. For postal shipments (USPS, international mail), this means customs fees are assessed and collected before delivery. For express carriers (DHL, FedEx, UPS), duties are typically collected from the recipient at time of delivery (or pre-billed to the shipper's account).

Classic AliExpress-to-Shopify dropshipping is fundamentally broken by this change. Here's the math:

A 10 percentage point drop in gross margin is devastating in a business model where ad costs are rising and margins were already thin. Many dropshippers running at 40–50% gross margin were barely profitable after ad spend. Losing another 10 points pushes many stores into unprofitability without price increases.

What Dropshippers Need to Do

  • Reprice immediately: Your cost basis has changed. Update pricing to reflect the new landed cost including duty.
  • Consider domestic sourcing or US warehouses: US-based 3PLs with inventory from domestic manufacturers avoid import duties entirely. Higher product cost, but no duty surprise.
  • Switch to Vietnam/India suppliers where feasible: For some product categories, equivalent suppliers exist in countries with lower tariff exposure. Still faces 10%, but better than 17.5–35%.
  • Bundle or consolidate orders: Fewer, larger shipments reduce per-unit brokerage overhead (though not the duty rate itself).
  • Communicate to customers: If duties are collected at delivery, customers may be confused or refuse packages. Pre-clear duties at checkout (DDP — Delivered Duty Paid) if your platform supports it.

Canada, EU, UK: How Does the US Change Compare?

The US had one of the most generous de minimis thresholds in the world. Most other major markets set much lower limits — meaning many foreign dropshippers already dealt with duties on small packages when selling into those markets.

Country / RegionDe Minimis ThresholdStatus
🇺🇸 United States$0 (was $800 USD)Suspended — duties apply on all shipments
🇨🇦 CanadaCAD $20 (~$15 USD)Active — duty-free only up to CAD $20
🇪🇺 European Union€150 (~$165 USD)Active — customs duty waived under €150; VAT always applies
🇬🇧 United Kingdom£135 (~$170 USD)Active — duty-free under £135; VAT always applies
🇦🇺 AustraliaAUD $1,000 (~$650 USD)Active — GST applies on all imports; duty waived under AUD $1,000

The contrast with Canada is striking. Canada's CAD $20 threshold (~$15 USD) means virtually every cross-border e-commerce package from the US or China pays duty when entering Canada. Canadian consumers already lived in a world the US is now entering.

The EU and UK are in the middle — €150/£135 covers many small consumer purchases but not all. Note that VAT always applies in the EU and UK regardless of the de minimis threshold, so even low-value packages from China owed VAT even before the US changed its rules.

How to Calculate Duty on Small Shipments Now

The calculation depends on your product and origin country. Here's a practical guide:

Step 1: Identify Your Product's HTS Code

The Harmonized Tariff Schedule code determines your base tariff rate. You can look up HTS codes at the USITC website. For most consumer goods from China, the relevant rates are:

  • Electronics (e.g., phone cases, cables, gadgets): MFN ~0–3.9% + Section 301 List 4A 7.5% + Section 122 10% = ~17.5–21%
  • Apparel and footwear: MFN ~12–67% + Section 301 List 4A 7.5% + Section 122 10% = can be very high
  • Toys and sporting goods: MFN ~0% + Section 301 List 4A 7.5% + Section 122 10% = ~17.5%
  • Home goods and furniture: MFN ~0–6% + Section 301 List 3 25% + Section 122 10% = ~35–41%

Step 2: Determine Your Declared Value

Duty is calculated on the transaction value — what the buyer actually paid, or a reasonable market value. Undervaluing packages is customs fraud and carries serious penalties. Use the actual price paid.

Step 3: Apply the Rate

Multiply the declared value by the applicable duty rate. That's your duty owed.

Example — $45 gadget from China:

ComponentAmount
Declared value$45
MFN rate (~0%)$0
Section 301 List 4A (7.5%)$3.38
Section 122 (10%)$4.50
Total duty owed$7.88

That $45 phone gadget now lands at your customer's door carrying an ~$8 customs bill. On a $45 sale, that's 17.5% of your product cost added in duty — if you're absorbing it as the seller, it comes straight out of margin.

Additional Fees to Budget For

  • CBP Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF): 0.3464% of value (minimum $32.71, maximum $634.62 per entry) for formal entries
  • Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF): 0.125% on ocean shipments
  • Brokerage fees: If a customs broker files on your behalf, expect $25–150+ per entry for informal entries

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is the de minimis $800 threshold really completely gone?
Yes — for now. Executive Order 14324 suspended duty-free de minimis treatment for all countries, and CBP has issued operational guidance (CSMS #66311990) for collecting duties on international mail. There is no minimum value below which packages automatically enter duty-free. That said, executive orders can be reversed, modified, or challenged. As of March 2026, duties apply to all small packages.
Q: What happens if a package arrives and I didn't pay duty upfront?
CBP or the postal carrier will hold the package and notify you (or the recipient) that duty is owed before release. For postal shipments, this may mean a notice card and a customs duty bill. For express carriers (DHL, FedEx, UPS), the carrier typically pays duty on behalf of the shipper and invoices them — this can come as a surprise bill weeks after delivery.
Q: Should I update my Shopify store pricing right now?
Yes, immediately if you're dropshipping from China or other international suppliers. Your landed cost has increased. If you're absorbing the duty (not passing it to customers), your margins have already taken the hit — even on orders already in transit. For new orders, price duty into your cost basis before calculating your selling price.
Q: Do non-China shipments also face duty now?
Yes. Executive Order 14324 suspended de minimis for all countries, not just China. Packages from Vietnam, India, Turkey, Bangladesh — anywhere — are now subject to duty. The rate will be lower for non-China goods (most face just the 10% Section 122 baseline plus MFN, which may be 0% for many items), but the automatic duty-free pass is gone across the board.
Q: Why did Temu pause US advertising?
Multiple sources indicate Temu paused US advertising on Meta (Facebook/Instagram) in 2025 due to the combination of the de minimis changes and elevated China tariffs making their direct-ship model economically unviable. Temu's entire US market strategy was built on direct-from-China shipments under the $800 threshold. When that loophole closed and China tariffs stayed elevated, the margin math collapsed. They've pivoted toward a US-warehouse model for some products, but the direct-ship volume from China has dropped dramatically.
Q: Is there any way to legally avoid these duties?
A few legitimate strategies: (1) Source from countries with lower tariff rates — Vietnam or India-origin goods face less total duty than China-origin goods. (2) Use a US-based 3PL or domestic manufacturer — products stored in a US warehouse and shipped domestically have no import duty. (3) Use a Bonded Warehouse or Foreign Trade Zone — goods can be stored duty-free until sold. (4) Check if your product qualifies for any preferential tariff treatment or has product-specific exclusions. None of these are loopholes — they're legitimate supply chain strategies.
Q: Will the de minimis exemption come back?
Possibly. Executive orders can be reversed. Congressional legislation could restore a threshold. Trade deals with specific countries could carve out exemptions. The Tax Foundation notes that the de minimis closure will raise ~$72.9 billion over 2026–2035 — so there's real fiscal pressure to keep it gone. But it was implemented by executive action, and it can be changed by executive action. Plan your business for the current reality while staying informed about changes.
