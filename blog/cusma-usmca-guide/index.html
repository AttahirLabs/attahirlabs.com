CUSMA/USMCA Explained: Does Your Product Qualify for Duty-Free Treatment?

If you're shipping goods between the US, Canada, and Mexico, you may be entitled to zero or reduced duties under CUSMA/USMCA — but only if your product actually qualifies. Here's how to know, how to certify, and what mistakes could cost you thousands.

What is CUSMA/USMCA?

CUSMA — the Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement — is the free trade agreement governing commerce between the three North American countries. It replaced NAFTA (the North American Free Trade Agreement) and entered into force on July 1, 2020.

The agreement goes by three different names depending on where you are:

CountryOfficial NameAbbreviation
🇨🇦 CanadaCanada-United States-Mexico AgreementCUSMA
🇺🇸 United StatesUnited States-Mexico-Canada AgreementUSMCA
🇲🇽 MexicoTratado entre México, Estados Unidos y CanadáT-MEC

Same agreement, different names. When a Canadian customs broker says "CUSMA" and a US freight forwarder says "USMCA," they mean the same thing.

The core benefit: goods that qualify as "originating" under the agreement can move between the three countries duty-free (or at a significantly reduced rate). For many ecommerce sellers and small importers, this is the difference between a viable margin and a loss.

In Canada, eligible shipments from the US receive the UST (United States Tariff) treatment. Shipments from Mexico receive MXT (Mexico Tariff) treatment. Both are listed in the CBSA's schedule of tariff treatments and typically result in a 0% duty rate on qualifying goods.

Source: Canada Border Services Agency (CBSA) — 2026 Customs Tariff

Does CUSMA Still Apply in 2026?

Yes — but with an important caveat you need to understand.

CUSMA itself is still fully in force. The agreement hasn't been suspended, renegotiated, or withdrawn. Qualifying goods moving between the US, Canada, and Mexico are still entitled to duty-free treatment under CUSMA in 2026.

However: Not all duties between these three countries are CUSMA duties. Other tariff regimes operate independently and can apply even to CUSMA-originating goods.

⚠ The Section 232 exception: Steel, aluminum, auto parts, and certain other products may face Section 232 tariffs even if they're CUSMA-originating. These are US national security tariffs and they don't disappear just because your product qualifies under CUSMA. Always check both your CUSMA eligibility and whether Section 232 applies separately.

The practical upshot for most importers: if you're shipping ordinary manufactured goods, consumer products, apparel, electronics, or food products between the three countries, CUSMA duty-free treatment applies as long as your product qualifies. The Section 232 carve-out primarily affects raw materials and vehicles.

Three Ways Your Product Qualifies

This is the core question: is your product "originating" under CUSMA? There are three pathways to qualification. Your product needs to meet at least one of them.

1. Wholly Obtained or Produced

The simplest category. Your product is entirely grown, extracted, or produced within the CUSMA region (US, Canada, and/or Mexico).

This applies to:

  • Agricultural products grown in the region (Canadian wheat, Mexican avocados, US corn)
  • Minerals and raw materials extracted from the region
  • Animals born and raised in the region
  • Fish caught in the territorial waters of the three countries
  • Goods made entirely from wholly-obtained materials

If a Canadian seller is shipping Quebec maple syrup to the US, this one's easy — it's wholly obtained in Canada, and it qualifies.

2. Tariff Classification Change (Substantial Transformation)

Your product contains some non-originating materials (materials sourced from outside the US/Canada/Mexico), but those materials have been substantially transformed through manufacturing within the region.

"Substantially transformed" means the manufacturing process changed the product's Harmonized System (HS) tariff classification at the chapter, heading, or subheading level — depending on the specific product rules.

Example: You import plastic pellets from South Korea (non-originating) into a Canadian facility and injection-mold them into a finished consumer product. The plastic pellets are in HS Chapter 39; the finished product might be in Chapter 84. That tariff classification change may satisfy the "tariff shift" rule, making the finished product CUSMA-originating.

The specific tariff shift rules vary by product and are laid out in CUSMA Annex 4-B (Product-Specific Rules). You need to look up your exact HS code to confirm.

3. Regional Value Content (RVC)

Your product contains non-originating materials, but you can demonstrate that enough of the product's value was created within the CUSMA region.

The RVC threshold varies by product and calculation method, but typically ranges from 40% to 60% of the product's value. There are two main calculation methods:

  • Transaction Value Method: The value of non-originating materials cannot exceed a set percentage of the transaction value
  • Net Cost Method: The originating content must represent a minimum percentage of net cost

The automotive sector has specific, stricter RVC rules: vehicles require 75% regional value content, and 40-45% of the vehicle content must come from workers earning at least USD $16/hour. This "labor value content" rule was new to CUSMA and doesn't exist in the old NAFTA.

✅ De minimis relief: If your product fails the tariff shift test due to a small amount of non-originating content, you may still qualify under the de minimis rule. Products can contain up to 10% non-originating content by value (calculated on the transaction value or total cost) and still be treated as originating — as long as the non-originating materials don't prevent the product from meeting other applicable rules. For textiles and apparel, the threshold is lower: 7% by weight.

How to Certify Origin

Knowing your product qualifies is only half the battle. You also need to properly certify its origin so the customs authority in the importing country can apply the preferential tariff treatment.

Under CUSMA, there is no official government form for origin certification — unlike under NAFTA, which used the Certificate of Origin (CO) form. This was deliberately simplified. Any document will work as long as it includes the required data elements.

Who Can Certify?

Three parties can certify origin under CUSMA:

  • The importer — based on their knowledge of the product's origin
  • The exporter — based on their knowledge of the product or a producer's certification
  • The producer — the manufacturer who has the best knowledge of inputs and transformation

Required Data Elements

Your certification must include all of the following:

#Required ElementNotes
1Certifier name, address, telephone, emailMust identify who is certifying (importer, exporter, or producer)
2Exporter name and addressIf certifier is not the exporter
3Producer name and addressIf known and different from exporter
4Importer name and addressIf known
5Description of goodsSufficient to identify the product
6HS tariff classification6-digit minimum
7Origin criterionWhich rule applies (A = wholly obtained, B = tariff shift, C = RVC, D = combination)
8Blanket periodIf covering multiple shipments (up to 12 months)
9Authorized signature and dateRequired
10Certifier statementMust attest the information is accurate and agrees to maintain records

The certification doesn't need to be a separate document — it can be incorporated into a commercial invoice. The CBSA and US CBP both accept certifications in any format as long as all data elements are present.

Common Mistakes That Cost Importers Money

1. Assuming CUSMA Applies Without Checking

Not every product that crosses the US-Canada border is automatically CUSMA-eligible. The origin rules are product-specific. A product assembled in Canada from Asian components may or may not qualify, depending on whether the tariff shift or RVC thresholds are met. Always verify before claiming the preference.

2. Missing the Certification on Import

Even if your product is genuinely CUSMA-originating, you won't get preferential treatment unless you claim it at import and have the certification to back it up. If you import regularly from a US or Mexican supplier and nobody has ever produced a CUSMA certification, you've likely been paying duties you didn't have to pay.

3. Shopify and Automated Label Systems Not Applying the Exemption

This is a real and underreported problem. A Canadian merchant posted to Reddit that they were hit with $7,500 in unexpected duties on goods they imported from a US supplier — goods that were legitimately CUSMA-eligible. The issue: their Shopify shipping labels and customs forms didn't include the CUSMA certification data or claim preferential tariff treatment. The shipments cleared as if no trade agreement existed.

Shopify's built-in customs declaration tools are designed for speed, not compliance precision. If you're not explicitly configuring your declarations to claim CUSMA/USMCA treatment and attaching a valid origin certification, you may be paying full MFN duties on goods that should cross duty-free.

⚠ Action item for Shopify merchants: Check the customs documentation attached to your last 3-5 cross-border shipments. Does it claim CUSMA/USMCA origin? If not, talk to your customs broker about amending recent entries and correcting your process going forward. CBSA allows amendments up to 4 years after the date of accounting.

4. Applying CUSMA to Products With Significant Third-Country Content

If your product contains substantial inputs from China, Southeast Asia, or other non-CUSMA countries, it may not qualify even if it was "made in the USA" or "assembled in Canada." The regional value content or tariff shift tests need to be satisfied. When in doubt, get a formal ruling from CBSA (for Canadian imports) or CBP (for US imports).

5. No Records to Support the Certification

Under CUSMA, the certifier must maintain records that support their origin certification for a minimum of 5 years (Canada) or 5 years (US). If CBSA or CBP audits you and you can't produce supplier invoices, bills of materials, or production records to substantiate your certification, you're liable for back duties, interest, and potential penalties.

6. Confusing CUSMA With Section 232 Exemptions

As mentioned above: CUSMA eligibility does not automatically exempt a product from Section 232 tariffs. If you're importing steel or aluminum products from a US supplier into Canada, CUSMA covers the Canadian MFN tariff. But if that steel originates in China and was only minimally processed in the US, it may still face Section 232 on the US side — and CUSMA may not apply at all if the product isn't truly US-originating.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What's the difference between CUSMA and NAFTA?
CUSMA replaced NAFTA on July 1, 2020. The core duty-free framework is similar, but CUSMA has stricter rules in several areas — particularly for automotive (the new 75% RVC threshold and $16/hour labor rule), stronger digital trade and intellectual property provisions, and a 16-year sunset clause (with a review every 6 years). The main practical change for most importers: there's no longer an official CO form — any document with the required data elements works.
Q: Does CUSMA still apply in 2026 despite tariff tensions?
Yes. The agreement is in force and neither Canada, the US, nor Mexico has withdrawn. Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, and autos apply separately and are not covered by CUSMA, but for most goods — consumer products, apparel, food, electronics, machinery — a CUSMA-qualifying product still moves duty-free across the border in 2026.
Q: What is the UST tariff treatment code in Canada?
UST stands for United States Tariff — the CBSA tariff treatment code applied to goods that qualify for CUSMA preference when imported into Canada from the United States. MXT (Mexico Tariff) is the equivalent for Mexican goods. Both result in a 0% duty rate for most goods. When you declare your shipment, you claim the UST or MXT treatment by providing a valid CUSMA origin certification.
Q: Does my product need a specific form to claim CUSMA treatment?
No. Unlike NAFTA, CUSMA does not require a specific form. Any document — a letter, an invoice annotation, a standalone certification — qualifies as long as it includes all 10 required data elements listed in the agreement. The CBSA publishes a template you can use, but it's not mandatory.
Q: I'm a small Shopify seller shipping from Canada to the US. Do I need to worry about CUSMA?
If your products are manufactured in Canada using Canadian inputs, they're likely CUSMA-originating and your US customers should qualify for duty-free import under USMCA. In practice, de minimis thresholds ($800 USD) cover most small orders — but for regular B2B shipments or higher-value orders, claiming USMCA treatment saves money. The bigger issue is the reverse: Canadian merchants importing US goods should be claiming CUSMA/UST treatment. Many aren't, and they're overpaying.
Q: What happens if I claim CUSMA treatment and I'm wrong?
You're liable for the unpaid duties, plus interest, and potentially penalties. In Canada, CBSA can assess additional duties going back up to 4 years. In the US, CBP can assess back to the date of entry. Fraudulent claims (knowingly certifying ineligible goods) carry significantly higher penalties. If you're uncertain, don't guess — ask your supplier for documentation, or request a formal advance ruling from CBSA or CBP before you start claiming the preference.
Q: What is the de minimis rule under CUSMA?
The de minimis rule allows a product to qualify as CUSMA-originating even if it contains a small amount of non-originating materials that don't meet the tariff shift test. The threshold is 10% of the transaction value or total cost for most goods. For textile and apparel products, the threshold is 7% by weight. This is especially useful for products with small imported components (hardware, fasteners, electronics) that would otherwise fail the tariff shift test on a technicality.
