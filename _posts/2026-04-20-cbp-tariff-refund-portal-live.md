If you saw headlines about a new tariff refund website going live, the short version is this: **yes, there is now a real refund process, and no, it’s not as simple as clicking a button and getting a wire two days later.**

U.S. Customs and Border Protection has launched Phase 1 of a new refund workflow for certain **IEEPA duties** through the ACE Portal, using a tool called **CAPE** (Consolidated Administration and Processing of Entries). That matters. A lot of businesses may have money tied up here.

But most of the coverage so far has been light on the part readers actually need: what qualifies, what doesn’t, what you need ready, and where to go without getting lost in CBP jargon.

That’s what this post is for.

## First, the official source

Start here, not on social media, not in a thread, not with some guy doing victory laps on LinkedIn:

- **CBP IEEPA Duty Refunds page:** [cbp.gov/trade/programs-administration/trade-remedies/ieepa-duty-refunds](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/programs-administration/trade-remedies/ieepa-duty-refunds)

That page is the main hub. CBP says they’ll keep updating it, and they probably will, because this is clearly being rolled out in stages.

## What changed

CBP says importers and authorized customs brokers can now submit certain refund requests for IEEPA duties through the **ACE Secure Data Portal** using a CSV upload called a **CAPE Declaration**.

A few important details:

- Phase 1 **went live April 20, 2026**
- the process is being rolled out in **phases**
- Phase 1 covers **some**, not all, refund scenarios
- CBP says valid refunds will generally be paid in **60 to 90 days after CAPE acceptance**, unless something needs further review

This is not ABI filing. It’s not entry-by-entry the old-fashioned way either. CBP is trying to batch and consolidate refunds so the process is less chaotic.

## What Phase 1 appears to cover

Based on CBP’s refund page and the CAPE guidance, Phase 1 is mainly aimed at:

- certain **unliquidated entries**
- certain entries **within 80 days of liquidation**
- some entries that are **extended, suspended, or under review**
- some **warehouse and warehouse withdrawal entries**

That sounds broad, but don’t get too excited yet. The exclusions matter just as much.

## What Phase 1 does **not** cover

This is the part a lot of articles bury.

At least in Phase 1, CBP says some entries will **not** be accepted through CAPE yet, including categories such as:

- entries flagged for **reconciliation**
- **drawback** entries
- entries covered by an **open protest**
- entries not filed in ACE or without a liquidation status in ACE
- some **AD/CVD-related** situations
- entries where liquidation is already final

So if someone told you, “Everybody can go get their tariff refunds now,” that’s sloppy at best.

## Who can submit the request

CBP says the CAPE Declaration can be filed by:

- the **Importer of Record (IOR)**, or
- the **authorized customs broker** who filed the entries for that importer

That means plenty of businesses are going to need to coordinate with their broker instead of trying to wing this alone.

## What you need before you do anything

This is the practical checklist.

### 1. Confirm you have ACE access

If you don’t already have the right ACE Portal access, start there:

- **Apply for an ACE Secure Data Portal account:** [cbp.gov/trade/automated/how-to-use-ace/portal-applying](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/automated/how-to-use-ace/portal-applying)
- **ACE Portal and ACH Refunds FAQs:** [cbp.gov/trade/automated/ace-portal-and-ach-refunds-faqs](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/automated/ace-portal-and-ach-refunds-faqs)

No ACE access, no real progress. That’s the bottleneck for a lot of people.

### 2. Make sure ACH refund setup is handled

CBP is paying these electronically. If your refund banking setup isn’t in place, fix that now.

Useful links:

- **ACH Refund information:** [cbp.gov/trade/automated/ach/refund](https://www.cbp.gov/trade/automated/ach/refund)
- **Electronic refund enrollment one-pager (CBP PDF):** [cbp.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/electronic-refund-enrollment-one-pager_508c.pdf](https://www.cbp.gov/sites/default/files/2026-04/electronic-refund-enrollment-one-pager_508c.pdf)

This part is boring. And it’s exactly the part people skip until they realize they’re not actually ready.

### 3. Figure out who should receive the refund

CBP’s materials refer to the importer, or a designated refund recipient / notify party.

Relevant links:

- **CBP Form 4811, Special Address Notification:** [cbp.gov/document/forms/form-4811-special-address-notification](https://www.cbp.gov/document/forms/form-4811-special-address-notification)
- **ACE Notify Party Information guidance:** [cbp.gov/document/guidance/ace-portal-notify-party-information-qrc](https://www.cbp.gov/document/guidance/ace-portal-notify-party-information-qrc)

If the broker, a designated affiliate, or some other party is supposed to receive the refund, don’t leave that fuzzy. Fuzzy is how money goes sideways.

### 4. Get your entry list together

CBP says the CAPE Declaration is basically a list of entry numbers in CSV format.

That sounds simple. Sometimes it is. Sometimes it means digging through a messy broker paper trail, old entry summaries, and internal shipping records while everyone insists they “definitely have it somewhere.”

You’ll want a clean list of potentially relevant entries before you touch the portal.

## What the filing actually looks like

At a high level:

1. The importer or authorized broker logs into the **ACE Portal**
2. They prepare a **CSV CAPE Declaration**
3. The file lists the entry numbers for which refunds are being requested
4. ACE validates the file and the entries
5. CBP updates the entry summary lines to remove the relevant IEEPA duties where accepted
6. CBP reviews, liquidates or reliquidates as needed, and then issues consolidated refunds

A few details worth remembering:

- each CAPE Declaration can include up to **9,999 entries**
- multiple CAPE Declarations can be filed
- CBP says no extra data beyond the required entry information in the CSV should be needed for the declaration itself

That’s cleaner than a lot of people expected. But cleaner doesn’t mean small businesses should assume this will be painless.

## The timing question everybody cares about

CBP says valid IEEPA refunds will **generally** be issued in **60 to 90 days following acceptance** of the CAPE Declaration.

That word, “generally,” is doing some real work.

If an entry is under review, suspended, extended, or otherwise messy, the refund path can still be slower. And if your records are a mess, you won’t even get to the “60 to 90 days” part quickly.

## What importers should do this week

If this affects your business, here’s the no-nonsense version.

### If you’re an importer

- confirm whether you or your broker will file
- confirm you have ACE access or know who does
- verify ACH refund enrollment status
- identify the entries that might qualify
- separate likely Phase 1 entries from everything more complicated

### If you rely on a customs broker

Send them a short, direct email today. Ask:

- Are our potentially refundable IEEPA entries identifiable now?
- Which of our entries appear eligible for Phase 1?
- Will you file CAPE Declarations on our behalf, or do we need to do anything inside ACE ourselves?
- Do we need to update refund recipient / notify party information?

Don’t ask vague stuff like “Can we get our tariff refunds?” You’ll get a vague answer back.

### If you’re still not sure whether you’re affected

Start by identifying:

- which imports were subject to the IEEPA duties in question
- when those entries were filed
- whether those entries are still within the current Phase 1 scope

If you can’t answer those three, you’re not at the filing step yet. You’re still at the “figure out your exposure” step.

## A blunt warning

A lot of businesses are going to underestimate the operations side of this.

The headlines make it sound like a refund portal just opened and now everybody gets paid back. That’s not really how this works. You still need the right entry data, the right portal access, the right party setup, and entries that actually fit the current phase.

So yes, there may be real money on the table. But if your customs records are a mess, CAPE is not going to magically fix that for you.

## Final thought

This is one of those updates where the existence of a portal is only half the story. The real question is whether your business has the records, portal access, and operational setup to use it without wasting a week getting bounced around.

If you do, there may be meaningful refunds at stake.

And if you don’t, the smartest move isn’t panic. It’s getting organized before the rest of the line forms.
