If you've been staring at your Shopify dashboard wondering what happened to your sales this month, you're not alone. March 2026 has been one of the slowest months merchants have seen since the early days of Covid. Threads on r/shopify are full of store owners reporting 30-50% drops compared to last year, and the pattern is eerily consistent across niches.

The good news: Q2 is around the corner. The bad news: if you're sitting on dead inventory when it arrives, you're tying up cash that should be funding your spring and summer pushes. Every week that unsold stock sits in your warehouse, it's costing you — in storage fees, in opportunity cost, and in the psychological weight of watching those units gather dust.

Here are five strategies that actually work for moving slow inventory, based on what we've seen working merchants do (and what the data supports).

## 1. Run a Time-Boxed Flash Sale — But Do It Right

Everyone knows flash sales work. The problem is most merchants run them badly. They slap a generic "SALE" banner on their homepage, discount everything 20%, and hope for the best. That's not a flash sale — that's a slow bleed on your margins.

Here's what actually moves dead stock:

**Make it specific.** Don't discount your whole catalog. Pick the 10-20 SKUs that haven't moved in 60+ days and create a dedicated collection for them. Give it a name that creates urgency: "Spring Cleanout — Gone by Friday" works better than "Sale Items."

**Make it short.** 48-72 hours maximum. The whole point of a flash sale is scarcity. If it runs for two weeks, it's just a price cut. Set a hard end date and stick to it.

**Make the discount real.** 15% off doesn't move dead stock. You need to hit the threshold where customers feel like they'd be stupid not to buy. For most products, that's 35-50% off. Yes, your margins hurt. But margin on inventory that never sells is zero.

**Use email, not just on-site banners.** Your existing customer list is the highest-converting audience you have. Send a dedicated email (not buried in a newsletter) with a clear subject line like "48 hours: 40% off these 12 items, then they're gone." Segment by past purchase behavior if you can — customers who bought similar products are the most likely buyers.

## 2. Bundle Dead Stock With Your Winners

This is the most underrated inventory strategy for Shopify stores. Take a slow-moving product and pair it with one of your best sellers at a slight discount on the bundle.

For example, if you sell skincare and your vitamin C serum flies off the shelves but your eye cream has been stagnant, create a "Complete Routine Bundle" that includes both at 15% off the combined price. The customer feels like they're getting a deal, you move the dead inventory, and your AOV goes up.

The key is to make the bundle feel intentional, not desperate. The slow-moving product should logically complement the popular one. Nobody wants a random grab bag — they want a curated set that makes their life easier.

**Pro tip:** Create 3-5 bundles at once and see which ones convert. Kill the losers after a week and double down on the winners. You can use Shopify's native bundling or any of the bundle apps on the App Store.

## 3. Liquidate Through Alternative Channels

Your Shopify store isn't the only place to sell. If a product isn't moving on your site, it might move somewhere else:

**Marketplace listings.** List your dead stock on Amazon, eBay, or Facebook Marketplace at aggressive prices. The goal isn't profit — it's cash recovery. Even selling at cost is better than holding inventory that depreciates to zero.

**Wholesale to complementary businesses.** If you sell direct-to-consumer, there might be brick-and-mortar stores or other online retailers in adjacent niches who'd buy your overstock at wholesale prices. A quick outreach to 20-30 relevant businesses on Instagram or LinkedIn can surprise you.

**B2B liquidation platforms.** Sites like Overstock.com's B-Stock, Liquidation.com, or even local liquidation buyers will take bulk inventory off your hands. The return is pennies on the dollar, but it's immediate cash and freed-up warehouse space.

**Donate for a tax write-off.** If the inventory is truly worthless at any price, donating it to a registered charity can give you a tax deduction. Talk to your accountant about the specifics, but for some merchants this is the smartest financial move for products that won't sell at any discount.

## 4. Turn Dead Stock Into a Marketing Asset

This one requires a mindset shift: stop thinking of dead stock as a problem and start thinking of it as a marketing budget.

**Free gift with purchase.** Set a minimum order value (say, $75) and offer a free product from your slow-moving inventory with every order that hits the threshold. This does three things: it increases your AOV, it moves dead stock, and it makes customers feel like they got a bonus. The "cost" is inventory you weren't going to sell anyway.

**Influencer seeding.** Send your slow-moving products to micro-influencers (1K-10K followers) in your niche. They get free product, you get content and exposure. If even one influencer post converts, you've turned dead stock into a customer acquisition channel.

**Giveaways and contests.** Run a social media giveaway using your dead stock as the prize. The engagement and follower growth can be worth more than the product value. Just make sure the giveaway is structured to attract your actual target audience, not just freebie hunters.

## 5. Get Systematic About Identifying Dead Stock Early

The best time to deal with dead inventory is before it becomes dead inventory. Most merchants don't have a clear system for flagging slow movers — they just notice when something's been sitting around for months.

Set up a weekly or biweekly review of your inventory velocity. Look at:

- **Days of inventory on hand** for each SKU. Anything above 90 days should be flagged.
- **Sales trend direction.** A product selling 10 units/week that drops to 2 units/week is about to become dead stock. Catch it early and you can discount at 20% instead of 50%.
- **Seasonal patterns.** If you sell winter gear and it's February, everything that hasn't sold needs to be moved *now*, not in March when it's truly dead.

If you're running a large catalog, doing this manually is painful. Tools like [StockClearance](https://apps.shopify.com/stock-clearance-manager) can automate the detection side — it flags products based on sales velocity and days on shelf, so you're not manually pulling reports every week. But even a simple spreadsheet with last-sold dates is better than nothing.

The point is to build a process, not just react when you realize you have a warehouse full of stuff nobody wants.

## The Bottom Line

March 2026 is rough, but it's not permanent. Consumer spending patterns are cyclical, and Q2 historically picks up as weather improves and spring/summer buying kicks in. The merchants who come out of this slump strongest will be the ones who used March to clean house — not the ones who sat on their inventory hoping it would sell itself.

Pick one or two strategies from this list and execute them this week. Don't over-plan, don't wait for the perfect moment. A flash sale launched tomorrow at 40% off will always outperform a perfectly planned campaign that launches "someday."

Your warehouse space and your cash flow will thank you.
